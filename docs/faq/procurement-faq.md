# B2B Procurement FAQ — 10 Real Quality Disputes and How to Resolve Them

> **Who this is for:** First-time and experienced paprika buyers who encounter quality questions during procurement. These are real scenarios that come up again and again in B2B spice trade.

---

## Q1: "The powder looks lighter than last time — is the quality inconsistent?"

**Short answer:** Batch-to-batch color variation of ±10–15 ASTA points is normal and expected. Your perception is influenced by lighting, background surface, and moisture content.

**Detailed explanation:**

Paprika color is determined by natural factors that vary by harvest:
- **Growing region weather:** A sunnier season produces higher carotenoid content
- **Harvest timing:** Late-harvest peppers tend to have deeper color
- **Cultivar:** Even the same field can have slight genetic variation between plants

The key metric is **ASTA color value**, not visual appearance. Two batches with the same ASTA value can appear different shades under different lighting (fluorescent vs daylight vs LED).

**What experienced buyers do:**
- Always compare using the lab standard (ASTA 20.1), not the naked eye
- Accept a ±15 ASTA point tolerance as normal between batches
- Request a pre-shipment retention sample sealed in a light-protected bag for later comparison
- For color-critical applications (e.g., sausage casing coloring), order a single large lot to avoid batch blending

---

## Q2: "My lab says ASTA 75 but your COA says ASTA 90 — which is correct?"

**Short answer:** This is the most common quality dispute in spice trade. In >80% of cases, the discrepancy is caused by **sampling and sample handling**, not a faulty product.

**Common causes of ASTA discrepancies:**

| Cause | Impact | Frequency |
|-------|:------:|:---------:|
| Sample exposed to light/heat during transit | 5–25% degradation | Very common |
| Different lab equipment calibration | ±3–5 ASTA | Common |
| Different test method (ASTA 20.1 vs ISO 7541) | ±1.5 ASTA | Rare (negligible) |
| Sample taken from surface layer only | Unrepresentative | Common |
| Product degraded during the voyage | Variable | Depending on conditions |

**Resolution protocol (recommended):**

1. **First check:** How was the sample stored from sampling to testing? If using a Ziploc bag at room temperature for 3+ days, the result is unreliable.
2. **Second check:** Request the seller's retention sample tested by an agreed third-party lab (SGS, Eurofins, Intertek).
3. **Third check:** If both labs disagree, ship a **new joint sample** from the sealed retention samples to a tie-breaker lab.
4. **Cost split:** Usually the party whose result is further from the specification pays for the third test.

## Q3: "The product has small clumps — is it expired?"

**Short answer:** Clumping does not necessarily mean expired. It often indicates **moisture migration** during transit, which can happen even with a fresh product.

**Clumping causes in order of likelihood:**

| Cause | Telltale Sign | Is the product still usable? |
|-------|--------------|:---------------------------:|
| Humidity absorption during transit | Clumps are hard but powder inside clumps is normal color | ✅ Yes — sift and use immediately |
| Temperature cycling + condensation | Clumps visible only on top layer bags | ✅ Yes — break clumps mechanically |
| Excessive moisture at production | Clumps throughout all bags uniformly | ⚠️ Test moisture content first |
| Age-related degradation | Clumps + color fading + off-odor | ❌ Reject |
| Pest/insect activity | Clumps with webbing or frass | ❌ Reject immediately |

**What to do:**
1. Test moisture content of the clumped material (should be ≤10%)
2. If moisture passes, pass through a 20-mesh sieve to break clumps
3. Test ASTA value — if still within spec, the product is usable with a note to use within 3 months
4. File a claim only if moisture exceeds spec or ASTA has degraded below minimum

## Q4: "There's a strange smell — is it spoiled?"

**Short answer:** Not necessarily. Paprika can develop different odors depending on the processing method, and some are normal.

**Odor identification guide:**

| Odor | Likely Cause | Action |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Sweet, fruity, or floral** | Normal high-quality paprika aroma | ✅ No action needed |
| **Earthy or hay-like** | Slight aging or drying method (sun-dried) | ✅ Normal for low-cost origins |
| **Smoky** | Dried over direct heat (common in some origins) | ⚠️ Check if customer's application is sensitive |
| **Sour or fermented** | Moisture >12%, microbial activity | ❌ Reject — do not use |
| **Musty/damp basement** | Storage in high humidity | ❌ Reject — potential mycotoxin risk |
| **Chemical/plastic** | Plasticizer migration from packaging | ⚠️ Air out product, test for off-flavor |
| **Burnt** | Over-drying or scorching during grinding | ⚠️ Accept if customer can tolerate flavor |
| **Rancid** | Oil oxidation (old stock, high temperature) | ❌ Reject — degraded quality |
| **No smell at all** | Sterilized (steam or irradiation) | ✅ Normal, flavor returns after cooking |

**Important:** If you smell sour, musty, or rancid, do not use the product. Request a replacement promptly.

## Q5: "The particle size is not what I ordered — it's too fine / too coarse"

**Short answer:** Particle size specifications use "passing through" language, which can cause misunderstandings. A "40 mesh" spec means 95% passes through a 40-mesh sieve — it does not mean the powder is all uniform 40-mesh particles.

**Particle size clarification:**

| Label | Typical Spec | Reality |
|-------|:-----------:|---------|
| 20 mesh coarse | 95% through 20 mesh | Particles range from 0.84 mm down to dust |
| 40 mesh standard | 95% through 40 mesh | Particles range from 0.42 mm down to dust |
| 60 mesh fine | 95% through 60 mesh | Particles range from 0.25 mm down to dust |
| 80 mesh extra fine | 95% through 80 mesh | Almost flour-like |

**Common misunderstandings:**
- "40 mesh" ≠ all particles are exactly 0.42 mm — that's physically impossible with grinding
- A finer grind can make the color appear **darker** due to increased surface light absorption — even with the same ASTA value
- A coarser grind gives slower color release in hot water/oil applications

**What to do:**
1. Request the exact sieve analysis report (ASTM E11 method)
2. For critical applications, arrange a pre-production sample with specific target particle distribution
3. If 5–10% of oversize is acceptable, state "99% through 40 mesh" instead of "95%"
4. Consider whether your application actually needs the strictest spec — many sausage makers accept 30–40 mesh range

## Q6: "There are black specks in the powder — is it contaminated?"

**Short answer:** Small dark specks are usually **pepper seed fragments** or **stem pieces**, not contamination. They're common in mid-to-low grade paprika and are cosmetically unappealing but harmless.

**Spot identification guide:**

| Appearance | Likely Identity | Is it a problem? |
|------------|----------------|:----------------:|
| Flat, dark brown/black, irregular shape | Seed fragment | No — naturally occurring |
| Thin, curved, dark green/brown | Stem or calyx piece | No — cosmetic only |
| Gritty, hard, crystalline | Sand or soil | ⚠️ Yes — test ash content (HCl insoluble) |
| Metallic, shiny, very small | Metal fragment | ❌ Yes — serious contamination |
| Uniform round dark dots | Insect frass | ❌ Yes — pest activity |

**Acceptable limits:**

| Contaminant | Premium | Superior | Standard | Special | Test Method |
|-------------|:-------:|:--------:|:--------:|:-------:|:-----------:|
| Seed fragments | ≤1% | ≤2% | ≤3% | ≤0.5% | Visual separation |
| Stem/calyx | ≤0.5% | ≤1% | ≤2% | ≤0.1% | Visual separation |
| Ash (total) | ≤8% | ≤8% | ≤10% | ≤8% | AOAC 923.03 |
| Ash (HCl insoluble) | ≤0.5% | ≤0.5% | ≤1% | ≤0.5% | AOAC 941.03 |

**What to do:**
1. Collect a representative sample from multiple bags
2. Visually separate dark specks and weigh them
3. If under spec limits, it's acceptable; if over, request replacement
4. For cosmetic-critical applications, specify "seedless" or "deseeded" in your PO

## Q7: "The COA says no Salmonella but I need more proof — what documents do I need?"

**Short answer:** A standard COA with a "Negative/25g" result is sufficient for most buyers. If your compliance team requires more, here is the documentation hierarchy.

**Documentation by confidence level:**

| Level | Documents | Use Case | Typical Cost |
|:-----:|-----------|----------|:-----------:|
| 1 | Supplier COA | Routine, low-risk orders | Free |
| 2 | Level 1 + Batch sterilization record | FSMA compliance, EU importers | Free (internal) |
| 3 | Level 2 + Third-party lab COA | First-time supplier, high-risk | $150–$300 |
| 4 | Level 3 + Environmental monitoring log | Large food manufacturer FSMA requirements | $500+ |
| 5 | Level 4 + Full FSSC/HACCP audit report | Private label, major retailer | $2,000+ |

**For US importers (FSMA requirements):**
- The **importer of record** must have a valid FSVP plan
- The supplier must have **foreign supplier verification** documentation
- Testing frequency depends on risk category — dried spices are generally considered "low risk" by FDA
- We recommend: Level 2 for recurring orders, Level 3 for first orders

**For EU importers (EC 2073/2005):**
- Salmonella must be **absent in 25g** (n=5, c=0 for Category 1 foods)
- Some member states require additional Enterobacteriaceae testing
- EU adopts a more risk-based approach — less documentation-heavy than US but stricter on results
