# Container Loading

## Standards-Based Definition

Container loading (container stuffing, vanning) is the systematic process of arranging, securing, and documenting packaged paprika products inside intermodal freight containers (ISO 668:2020 — Series 1 freight containers) for international transport, in compliance with the IMO/ILO/UNECE Code of Practice for Packing of Cargo Transport Units (CTU Code), cargo securing guidelines (EN 12195-1:2010), and food-safe transport regulations (EU Regulation 852/2004 Annex II, Chapter IV).

## Overview

Container loading is the critical interface between product preservation and logistics execution. A well-executed loading plan ensures that paprika arrives at the destination in the same condition as it left the factory — stable pallets, intact packaging, no moisture damage, and no contamination. Failure in container loading is the #3 cause of spice arrival quality complaints (after processing defects and storage issues), accounting for approximately 12–18% of quality-related claims. The financial impact of a single damaged container of premium paprika can range from $5,000 (minor moisture spotting) to $50,000+ (total condemnation). Loading procedures must account for the specific vulnerabilities of paprika: moisture sensitivity, compressibility of powder, and the risk of taint absorption.

### Critical Loading Procedures

| Procedure | Specification | Purpose | Verification Method |
|-----------|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| Container Pre-inspection | Clean, dry, odor-free, no damage, food-grade | Prevent contamination | Visual + olfactory check (use flashlight) |
| Container Liner Installation | 0.15–0.20 mm PE liner, full container length | Moisture barrier, hygiene | Visual inspection of integrity |
| Floor Protection | Cardboard or corrugated sheets on floor | Absorb condensation, protect bags | Visual |
| Desiccant Placement | 2–5 kg for 40' HC: overhead strip or bags | Condensation control | Count desiccant units; position at 12 placement points |
| Pallet Alignment | <5 cm gap between pallets; no tilting | Stability | Measure gaps, check vertical alignment |
| Gap Filling | Inflatable dunnage bags (4–6 per container) | Prevent lateral shifting | Pressure check (0.15–0.25 bar for poly bags) |
| Stretch Wrapping | 70–100% pre-stretch, 5–7 layers, overlap >50% | Pallet unitization | Visual — wrap covers >80% of pallet height |
| Corner Boards / Edge Protectors | 5 mm L-board on each vertical edge | Prevent strap damage | Visual |
| Securing Straps | 2–4 horizontal straps per row | Load consolidation | Tension check |
| Container Seal | High-security bolt seal (ISO 17712) | Tamper evidence | Seal number matches shipping documents |
| Weight Distribution | 60% forward, 40% aft; axle load <12,000 kg (truck) | Road/container stability | Weighbridge certificate |

### Condensation and Moisture Management

| Climate Zone (Route) | Risk Level | Recommended Actions | Desiccant Requirement |
|---------------------|------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Temperate → Temperate | Low | Standard liner + corner boards | 1–2 kg desiccant |
| Temperate → Tropical | High | Liner + active desiccant + moisture barrier bags | 3–5 kg desiccant |
| Tropical → Temperate | Very High | Maximum measures: reefer with humidity control | 5+ kg + silica gel at 5–10 g/kg |
| Trans-Equatorial | Extreme | Reefer at 15–18°C, humidity loggers inside cargo | 5+ kg + do not use standard containers |
| Asia → Europe (winter) | Moderate | Liner + desiccant | 2–3 kg |
| Asia → Europe (summer) | High | Liner + full desiccant + moisture-resistant packaging | 4–5 kg |

### Loading Inspection Checklist (Pre-Stuffing)

1. **Container hygiene:** any residual odor (chemical, mold, animal)? → Reject container
2. **Floor condition:** cracks, splinters, protruding nails? → Repair or reject
3. **Wall/door condition:** panels intact, door mechanism functional
4. **Ventilation settings:** closed (standard) or open per instruction
5. **CSC plate valid:** Container Safety Convention, inspection not expired
6. **Temperature record:** if reefer, verify set point and pre-cooling (2 hours at set point before loading)
7. **Packaging condition:** no tears, no moisture, no pest evidence on packed product

## Industrial & Commercial Importance

- **Arrival Quality:** Proper loading can reduce arrival moisture damage by 60–80% versus poor loading. For every 1% moisture increase during transit, ASTA declines approximately 5–8 points.
- **Claims Prevention:** Container-related claims account for 12–18% of spice trade disputes. Documented loading procedures (with photographs) are the primary evidence in cargo claims.
- **Regulatory Compliance:** EU Regulation 852/2004 requires that food transport containers be clean, maintained in good condition, and protect food from contamination. Failing these standards can result in border rejection.
- **Cost Optimization:** A 40' HC container of paprika at 26 MT vs. 22 MT loading density represents a $0.15–0.20/kg freight cost difference. Proper planning maximizes container utilization.

## Application Guidance for Procurement & QC

1. **Require a container loading report** with photographs (minimum 6 photos: empty container, liner installation, desiccant placement, first pallet, full load, seal applied) as part of shipping documentation.
2. **Specify container type and condition** in the sales contract: e.g., "Container: 40' HC, clean and food grade, with PE liner, desiccant at 4 kg minimum. No used chemical containers."
3. **Install temperature/humidity data loggers** inside the cargo for sensitive premium shipments. Download and review upon arrival.
4. **Request VGM certificate** before vessel departure — mismatch between declared and actual weight causes port detention fees and potential vessel stability issues.
5. **Inspect for container sweat damage** upon arrival: check top bags in each pallet for moisture spots. Photograph and report within 72 hours for insurance claim eligibility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is "container sweat" and how does it damage paprika?**
A: Container sweat is condensation that forms on the steel ceiling and walls when the container transitions between temperature zones (e.g., subtropical loading → equatorial → temperate). The cold container surfaces cause moisture in the warm air to condense. Condensation drips onto the top layers of bags, wetting the surface — this can penetrate through paper bags, increasing moisture content at contact points from 8% to 20%+. Affected product must be sorted out (5–15% of container contents typically). Mitigation: container liner (absorbs condensation on the plastic surface, directing it to the floor), desiccants (absorb up to 300% of their weight in water vapor), and avoiding extreme temperature routes during high-risk seasons.

**Q: How many desiccant units should be placed in a 40' HC container of paprika?**
A: The standard calculation for moisture-sensitive cargo: 1 CTU (moisture absorption unit = 1 kg silica gel) covers approximately 4–6 m³ of container headspace. For a 40' HC container (76.3 m³), use 5–8 kg desiccant, distributed: 2–3 units overhead (suspended from cargo lashing rings), 2–3 units on the floor between pallet rows, and 1–2 units near the door area. For high-risk routes (tropical to temperate), double the quantity. Each desiccant bag should be placed away from direct contact with bags (use a sealed tray or mesh separation).

**Q: What is the difference between FCL (Full Container Load) and LCL (Less than Container Load) for paprika?**
A: FCL = the buyer rents the entire container; the supplier fills it. Advantages: exclusive use (no cross-contamination risk from other cargoes), faster transit (no consolidation/deconsolidation), and lower per-unit cost at full container volume. LCL = sharing container space with other shipments. Disadvantages: higher risk of taint contamination (paprika absorbs odors from spices, chemicals), longer transit (consolidation/deconsolidation adds 3–7 days), and higher per-kg freight cost (typically 20–40% more than FCL for equivalent volumes). Recommendation: always use FCL for paprika unless the volume is clearly <15 m³.

**Q: How do I file a claim for container-damaged paprika upon arrival?**
A: Step 1 — Document: photograph all damage within 72 hours of container opening. Step 2 — Notify carrier in writing (email + formal letter) within 3 days of delivery (carrier time limits are strict). Step 3 — Request joint survey with carrier's surveyor or independent surveyor (Bureau Veritas, SGS, Cotecna). Step 4 — Quantify: sort damaged from undamaged product, test moisture content of damaged bags (ISO 939), estimate ASTM loss. Step 5 — File claim: provide survey report, photos, container loading report, B/L, and commercial invoice. Claims exceeding $5,000 typically go through marine cargo insurance (buyer's or seller's policy, depending on Incoterm).

**Q: Can moisture damage during shipping be avoided by using refrigerated containers (reefers)?**
A: Reefers reduce but do not eliminate moisture risk. A reefer set at 18°C with controlled humidity (50–60% RH) is the gold standard for paprika and eliminates most sweat damage. However: (1) reefers cost $500–1,500 more per container than dry containers, (2) the temperature differential between reefer air and cargo mass can still cause condensation if product is loaded warm, (3) reefer vents must be set to "fully fresh air" or "60/40" to prevent CO₂ buildup (paprika powder continues to respire slowly). For premium paprika ($5+/kg FOB), reefer cost is typically justified.

**Q: What pallet configuration does Dinweys use as standard for container loading?**
A: Dinweys' standard export configuration for paprika powder: 25 kg multi-layer kraft bags (with PE liner), stacked 9 layers high on 1,100 × 1,100 mm ISO pallets (45 bags/pallet, 1,125 kg gross), stretch-wrapped with 7 layers of 20 µm pre-stretched film, corner boards on 4 vertical edges. A 40' HC container holds 20 pallets (900 bags = 22.5 MT). Each pallet is separated by at least 1 inflatable dunnage bag per row (6 total per container). Desiccant at 4 kg is standard; upgradeable to 8 kg for tropical destinations. A comprehensive container loading report with time-stamped photographs is provided for every shipment.
